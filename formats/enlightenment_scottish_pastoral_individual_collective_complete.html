Chapter 1

The ewe was dying on her back, a great woolly beetle helpless in the dew-drenched grass of the high field. Her legs paddled the cold air, a slow, desperate rhythm against the dawn. Finlay Munro saw her from two hundred yards off, a white blotch gone wrong against the green, and he broke into a run, the old dog, Tam, flowing ahead of him like grey smoke.

“Ach, damn it. Damn it all.”

The words were less curse than punctuation, a breath-steam in the chill. The hill rose to meet him, the ground sucking at his boots. He felt the familiar protest in his knees, the tightness across his shoulders, but he pushed through it, the rhythm of the run a second heartbeat. This was the motion of his life: towards the thing that needed fixing, or burying. The sky was a vast, pale bowl, bleeding pink at its eastern rim. Below, the strath was still drowned in night’s blue shadow, the loch a slab of dull iron. Up here, the light was clean and merciless.

He reached her. Tam circled, whining low in his throat. The ewe’s eye, a slick black marble, rolled to meet Finlay’s. There was no panic in it, just a deep, weary resignation. Her belly was a taut drum, the lambs inside shifting, a life that was now a death sentence. Bloat. She’d gone over, likely in the night, and the gasses had built up, trapping her. A stupid, mundane end.

“Right, you,” Finlay murmured, his voice dropping into the soft, steady register he used for broken things. He knelt, the wet soaking instantly through his trousers. He placed a hand on her heaving flank, feeling the unnatural tightness. “Let’s have a wee go.”

He worked quickly, his fingers finding the spot on her left side, high up. From his pocket he took the short, sharp blade, wiped it on his sleeve. A quick stab, a hiss of escaping gas like a sigh from the earth itself. The tension in her belly collapsed. He withdrew the blade, plugged the tiny wound with a dab of antiseptic from a tube. Then, with a grunt that was part effort, part sympathy, he rolled her. For a moment she lay prone, stunned. Then her legs gathered under her. She lurched up, staggered, shook herself as if waking from a bad dream, and coughed. She took a few steps, looked back at him with that same opaque stare, then dropped her head to graze, as if the whole ridiculous tragedy had never occurred.

Finlay sat back on his heels, wiping the blade clean on the grass. The crisis was past, but the aftermath hummed in his hands. He was fifty-seven. His hands were broad, the knuckles swollen, the skin cross-hatched with a hundred fine white scars from wire, teeth, and tools. They were a map of this place. He looked at them, then out across the land.

His land. The thought arrived, as it always did, with a weight that was neither pride nor burden, but a simple, exhausting fact. The Munro tenancy of Glen Cùil stretched over three thousand acres of rough pasture, birch scrub, heather hill, and peat bog. It was not beautiful in the postcard sense. It was bony and demanding, its beauty a severe, transactional thing: light on the distant peaks, the sudden green of a hidden spring, the curlew’s call that could slice your heart open if you weren’t careful. It was a beauty that asked for sweat in return.

Tam nudged his hand. Finlay scratched the dog’s ears, the rough warmth a comfort. “Aye, we’re done here.” He stood, his knees cracking like dry twigs. The ewe would likely be fine. The lambs might yet come. It was a reprieve, not a victory. Victories were rare here; you measured life in reprieves and managed losses.

He turned for home, the long, low stone house visible now as the sun lipped the hills, turning its windows to gold. Smoke threaded from the chimney. Morag was up. The day proper was beginning.

The track down was stony, familiar to his feet in the dark. He could hear the burn chuckling to his left, swollen with the overnight rain. The air smelled of wet earth, peat smoke, and the faint, sweet rot of last year’s bracken. As he walked, he took inventory, not with his eyes, but with his bones. The fence by the big birch needed a new post. The roof of the byre was getting patchy. The tractor was making that noise again, the one that sounded like a spoon in a tin can. A list, endless and cyclical, etched on the wind.

He pushed open the kitchen door, and the world shifted from vastness to intimacy in a single step. Heat, the smell of frying bacon, baking bread, and the underlying, permanent scent of woodsmoke and damp wool.

Morag stood at the Aga, her back to him. She was a small woman, made smaller by a slight stoop, but she commanded the warmth of the room. Her hair, more silver than black now, was twisted into a loose knot. She didn’t turn.

“Saved one,” he said, hanging his jacket on the peg.

“I heard you running. Bloat?”

“Aye.”

“And the lambs?”

“Time will tell.”

She turned then, holding a spatula. Her face was lined, not delicately, but deeply, as if the weather and the worry had carved their own geography. Her eyes were the colour of the slate in the burn. “You’re soaked. Breakfast is near ready. Call the lass.”

Finlay moved to the bottom of the stairs. “Eilidh!” His voice, too loud for the kitchen, echoed in the stone stairwell.

A muffled sound, a thump, then the sound of feet. Eilidh appeared, seventeen, a sleep-tousled version of her mother with her father’s height. She was wrapped in a huge, faded hoodie, her phone glowing in her hand. She glanced at it, then at him.

“You’re all wet.”

“Astute. The world is wet. Come and eat.”

They sat at the scrubbed pine table. The radio, an ancient thing with a cracked case, muttered about politics in a faraway city. Morag served them: bacon, eggs, black pudding, fried bread. A feast, every morning. It was necessary fuel. They ate in a silence that was not empty, but full of the day’s anticipations.

Eilidh broke it, her eyes still on her phone. “Jamie’s dad is taking a load of store lambs to the market at Stirling next week. He says he could take ours too. Save us the trip.”

Finlay chewed slowly. Jamie’s dad, Alastair Grant, farmed over the hill. He had newer machinery, a bigger truck, a louder voice. “Our lambs aren’t ready.”

“They will be. He’s offering.”

“I’ll decide when they’re ready. And I’ll take them.”

Eilidh shrugged, a gesture of profound detachment. “Just saying. It’s efficient.”

“Efficiency,” Finlay said, “is not the point.”

“What is the point, then? Doing everything the hard way?”

“Eilidh,” Morag said, her tone a low warning.

Finlay looked at his daughter. She saw the farm as a series of problems to be solved, inputs and outputs. She was clever, doing some business course at the college in Inverness two days a week. She talked about “diversification” and “value-added products.” He saw the same land as a living, breathing entity you made a covenant with. You didn’t diversify a covenant. You kept it.

“The point,” he said, his voice calm, “is knowing your own stock. Seeing them through. A man who just trucks them doesn’t know which hogs are strong, which ewes are good mothers. He just sees weight and price. That’s how you lose the thread.”

“The thread,” Eilidh repeated, not quite mocking, but not understanding either. “Right.”

After breakfast, Finlay went out to the yard. The day had settled into a cool, bright clarity. He was mucking out the byre, the rhythmic scrape of the fork, the sweet, pungent smell of manure and old hay, when he saw the car.

It was a sleek, grey thing, alien against the mud and stone. It picked its way carefully down the track, too clean, too quiet. It stopped by the gate. A man got out. He was in his forties, dressed in the urban-casual uniform of expensive waterproofs and clean boots that had never been broken in. He had a tablet under his arm.

Finlay leaned on his fork. Tam, at his feet, gave a low growl.

The man approached with a careful smile. “Mr. Munro? Finlay Munro?”

“I am.”

“David Thornbridge. From Cairn Solutions.” He offered a hand. Finlay looked at his own, gloved and dirty, and made no move to take it off. The man, Thornbridge, withdrew his hand smoothly. “Sorry to drop in unannounced. I did send a letter.”

“I get a lot of letters. They burn well.”

Thornbridge’s smile tightened. “Of course. Well, I’m here in person now. Do you have a moment?”

“I’m in the middle of a moment.”

“Right. I’ll be brief. Cairn Solutions represents a consortium of investors. We’re putting together a land management project in this glen. A coordinated, ecologically-sensitive rewilding initiative. We’re approaching all the tenants and landowners.”

Finlay said nothing. The word ‘rewilding’ hung in the air between them, glossy and foreign.

“The idea,” Thornbridge continued, undeterred, “is to restore the landscape to its natural, pre-anthropogenic state. Reduce grazing pressure, reintroduce native woodland, facilitate species recovery. It’s about healing the land, Mr. Munro. Letting it breathe again.”

Finlay looked past him, at his land. The sheep dotted the lower field. His grandfather had shepherded those hills. His father had drained the boggy patch by the burn to make the hayfield. This man saw ‘grazing pressure.’ Finlay saw the careful, generations-old negotiation between beast and grass, man and soil.

“Letting it breathe,” Finlay repeated. “And my sheep? My breath?”

Thornbridge’s expression was one of polite pity. “There would be compensation, of course. A generous buy-out of your tenancy rights. You’d be free of the… the grind of it. And you’d be contributing to something larger. A collective vision for the glen.”

Collective. The word tasted of nothing. A collective of people who didn’t live here, making a vision for a land they didn’t understand.

“This collective,” Finlay said. “Would they be the ones getting up at four to pull a lamb in a sleet storm? Or watching a good cow die for no reason you can see? Would they be the ones whose hands are in this dirt?”

“It’s a different kind of stewardship, Mr. Munro. Science-led. Holistic.”

“My stewardship is plenty holistic. It includes my family’s supper.”

Thornbridge sighed, a patient, worldly sound. “Change is coming to these glens, whether we like it or not. This is a chance to be part of it, on favourable terms. To leave a legacy.”

Finlay straightened up. He pulled off his glove, finally, and wiped his forehead with the back of his wrist. He met Thornbridge’s eye. “My legacy is right here. It’s not for sale as part of a… consortium’s vision.”

“It’s not just about you, though, is it?” Thornbridge said, his voice softening into a weapon. “There’s the future to consider. Your daughter’s future. Does she want this life? Really?”

A cold anger, quiet and deep, stirred in Finlay’s gut. He took a step forward. Tam, sensing the shift, stood up, his hackles a subtle ridge along his back.

“You should go now,” Finlay said, his voice low. “You’re frightening my dog.”

Thornbridge blinked. He looked at Tam, then back at Finlay. The polite mask slipped for a second, revealing something harder, more calculating. “We’ll be in touch, Mr. Munro. The offer won’t be on the table forever. Think about the collective good.”

He turned and walked back to his grey car. It made a soft, expensive purr as it turned and crept back up the track.

Finlay stood watching until it was gone. The silence rushed back in, but it was different now. It had a new shape, with a thornbridge-shaped hole in it. He looked at the hills, the sheep, the smoke from his chimney. The words hung in the clean air, poisoning it.

Rewilding. Holistic. Collective good.

He spat on the ground, a brief, sharp punctuation. Then he went back to mucking out the byre. The work was the same. The fork felt the same in his hands. But the day had tilted, just a degree. Enough to feel the slide.

Chapter 2

The stone of the kirkyard wall was sun-warmed against Finlay’s back. It was Sunday afternoon, the Sabbath work of morning chores done, the secular quiet of the day settling in. He sat with his eyes closed, face turned to the light, listening to the gossip of starlings in the yew tree and the distant, rhythmic thock of axe on wood from behind the house. Calum, his younger brother, was splitting logs. The sound was a heartbeat.

This was the hour of ceasefire. The hour when the land was not a taskmaster, but a companion. He could hear Morag inside, the faint clatter of dishes. Eilidh was upstairs, likely lost in the bright, noisy world of her screen. Here, against the old wall with its lichen maps, he could almost believe the man in the grey car had been a dream.

Almost.

“He’s got the Grant place, you know.”

Finlay didn’t open his eyes. He knew the voice: old Hector MacLeod, from the croft at the head of the loch. Hector was eighty if he was a day, a stalk of a man with a face like a weathered knot. He appeared at the gate as if conjured by the thought of trouble.

“Who has?” Finlay asked, though he knew.

“Your man with the shiny motor. Thorn-bloody-bridge.” Hector leaned on the gate, his breathing a faint whistle. “Was up at Alastair Grant’s yesterday. Stayed two hours. Came out smiling. Alastair came out looking like he’d swallowed a wasp, but he was smiling too. The kind of smile you have when someone’s just told you the value of your own soul, and it’s less than you hoped but more than you deserve.”

Finlay opened his eyes. The sky was a profound, aching blue. “Alastair’s always been a businessman first, a farmer second.”

“Aye, well. The business is changing.” Hector pushed the gate open and shuffled in, sitting heavily on the wall a few feet away. He took out a pipe, began the slow ritual of filling it. “They’re not just buying sheep, Fin. They’re buying silence. They’re buying the view. They’re buying us out of the picture so the picture looks prettier to the folk who’ll never get mud on their boots looking at it.”

“He came here. I told him no.”

Hector struck a match, the flare bright in the shade. He sucked the pipe to life, a small, fragrant cloud blooming around his head. “You did. And you’ll be a stone in their shoe now. They want the whole glen, see? A clean slate. Your ‘no’ makes the slate dirty. They don’t like dirt.”

The thock-thock from the woodpile stopped. Calum appeared around the corner of the house, axe over his shoulder, his shirt dark with sweat. At fifty, he was built like a barrel, stronger than any man had a right to be, with a quietness that could be mistaken for simplicity. It wasn’t. He nodded at Hector.

“Talking about the southern invader?” Calum’s voice was a gravelly rumble.

“Aye,” said Hector.

Calum leaned the axe against the wall. “Saw his car down at the old McPhail place last week. Place has been empty five years. He wasn’t there for the scenery.”

A cold knot tightened in Finlay’s stomach. The McPhail croft was a ruin, but it had grazing rights attached, rights that wove through the heart of the Munro tenancy like a thread in a plaid. If someone owned that croft, they could cause chaos.

“What’s their offer?” Calum asked, looking at Finlay.

“Didn’t get that far. Didn’t care to.”

“You should have listened,” Calum said, not critically, just stating a fact. “You can’t fight what you don’t understand.”

“I understand enough. They want us gone. They want to plant trees and bring back wolves and charge people from the city to feel spiritual about it.”

“Wolves pay better than sheep,” Calum said, a dark joke glinting in his eyes.

“This isn’t a joke, Cal.”

“I know it’s not.” Calum wiped his face with his forearm. “But Hector’s right. You said no. So now we’re the resistance. And we don’t know what we’re resisting, or how big it is.”

The three men sat in silence. The starlings quarrelled. From inside, Morag started singing a psalm, her voice faint and true through the open window. I to the hills will lift mine eyes… It was the old version, the metrical one. A sound from childhood.

Hector took his pipe from his mouth. “There’s a meeting. Thursday night. Village hall.”

“What meeting?” Finlay asked.

“About the ‘future of the glen.’ Called by the community council. But I’d bet my last tin of tobacco your man Thornbridge is the ghost in the machine. He’ll be there, with his tablet and his pretty pictures of trees. And Alastair Grant will be there, nodding like a puppet. They’ll talk about grants, and biodiversity credits, and green prosperity. They’ll make it sound like we’re backward, holding on to the past while they sail into the glorious, wild future.”

“And what do we do?” Calum asked.

“We go,” Hector said. “We listen. And then we speak. With our boots on and our hands in plain view.”

After Hector had shuffled off and Calum had returned to his woodpile, Finlay went inside. The kitchen was cool, dim after the bright yard. Morag was at the table, her hands busy shelling peas into a colander. The ping of them hitting the metal was a peaceful sound.

“Hector’s worried,” she said, not looking up.

“Hector’s always worried. It’s his hobby.”

“This is different.” She looked at him then, her slate-grey eyes seeing everything. “He’s right to be. I had Mairi Grant on the phone. Alastair’s signed something. An ‘option,’ she called it. She was crying. Said he didn’t even talk to her about it. Just came in and said they were set for life.”

Finlay pulled out a chair and sat heavily. The solidity of the old oak under him was a comfort. “An option to sell the tenancy?”

“To sell their interest. Whatever that means. It means they’re gone, Fin. As soon as the last lamb is sold.”

He thought of Alastair Grant, a big, red-faced man who loved a deal more than he loved his own land. He’d sell the sky if he could get a price for the blue. But it was a crack in the dam. If the Grants went, the logic of the glen changed. The Munro holding would be an island in a sea of… what? Managed wilderness? A playground?

“We’re not selling,” he said, the words meant for himself as much as for her.

“I know.” Her fingers never stopped, the peas pinging, pinging. “But ‘no’ might not be a word they understand. Their language is money and paper. Ours is… this.” She gestured with a pea-pod towards the window, the fields, the hills. “I’m not sure there’s a translation.”

Eilidh came in then, headphones around her neck, the tinny beat of music leaking out. She went to the fridge, took out a bottle of water.

“Uncle Cal says there’s a big meeting Thursday,” she said, leaning against the counter. “About some eco-project.”

“It’s not an eco-project,” Finlay said, the heat in his voice surprising him. “It’s a clearance. A polite, twenty-first century clearance.”

Eilidh took a swig of water. “Jamie says his dad says it could be really good for the area. Bring in funding. Create different kinds of jobs. Maybe even tourism that isn’t just people taking pictures of sheep.”

“Jamie’s dad has sold his soul and his stock,” Finlay snapped. “And Jamie will be off to university in Glasgow in the autumn, so it’s easy for him to have opinions on what’s good for an area he’s leaving.”

Eilidh’s face closed. “Maybe people leave because there’s nothing to stay for. Just hard work and no future.”

The kitchen went very quiet. The only sound was Morag’s peas, a frantic, brittle percussion now.

“Is that what you think?” Finlay asked, his voice dangerously soft.

Eilidh looked at him, then at her mother, then at the floor. “I think… I think it’s complicated. That’s all.” She pushed off from the counter and left, her footsteps quick on the stairs.

The silence she left behind was thick and hurting. Morag stopped shelling. She laid her hands flat on the table, looking at the green stains on her fingers.

“She’s young, Fin. She’s looking for a door. Any door. This place can feel like a wall.”

“It’s not a wall. It’s a home.”

“It can be both.”

That night, Finlay lay awake beside Morag’s sleeping form. The moon was high, casting a silver wash through the window, painting the familiar contours of their room in alien light. He thought of the ewe that morning, on her back, legs paddling at the empty air. The feeling of helplessness. The simple, physical act of rolling her over, of giving her ground beneath her feet again.

What was the equivalent act now? What pin-prick would deflate this pressure building in the glen?

He thought of his hands on the fork, on the ewe, on the tractor wheel. They were tools for fixing, for holding, for doing. They were useless against words like ‘option’ and ‘consortium’ and ‘holistic vision.’

Morag stirred in her sleep, murmured something unintelligible. He reached out in the dark and found her hand, calloused and small in his. He held it, this anchor, and stared at the ceiling until the moon moved and the room sank back into a kinder darkness.

The resistance, Hector had called it. But resistance required a target, a plan. All Finlay had was a deep, unshakable no that felt less like a stance and more like the last reflex of a creature that knows only one way to be.

He closed his eyes. In the theatre of his mind, he saw the grey car, moving silently from farm to croft to ruin, a spider weaving a web. And he saw himself, a fly in the centre, feeling the vibrations, knowing the web was there, but unable to see its full, glistening, intricate shape.

Chapter 3

The village hall smelled of floor polish, damp wool, and decades of weak tea. Folding chairs were set in uneven rows, facing a trestle table where three men sat: the community council chairman, old Mr. Ferguson, who was mostly deaf; Alastair Grant, looking uncharacteristically solemn in a checked shirt; and David Thornbridge, in a soft charcoal sweater, his tablet glowing before him like a secular altar.

Finlay stood at the back, near the fire exit, with Calum a solid presence beside him. Hector was in the front row, his pipe unlit but clenched in his teeth like a weapon. The hall was three-quarters full – a big turnout for a Thursday. Faces he’d known all his life: other tenants, a few crofters, the postmistress, the owner of the struggling bunkhouse. Their expressions were a mix of curiosity, suspicion, and a deep-seated wariness.

Mr. Ferguson tapped the microphone, producing a thump that made everyone wince. “Right, then. We’re here tae discuss… future opportunities for the glen. Mr. Thornbridge has asked tae say a few words. He’s from… a company.” He gestured vaguely.

Thornbridge stood. He had a practised, easy smile. “Thank you, Mr. Ferguson. And thank you all for coming. I know your time is precious.” His voice was smooth, amplified just enough to be clear without being aggressive. “My name is David Thornbridge, and I represent Cairn Solutions. We’re not a distant corporation. We’re a partnership of conservationists, ecologists, and yes, some investors who believe in putting their money where their values are. And we believe in the future of this glen.”

He tapped his tablet, and a large, stunning image appeared on the white wall behind him: a panoramic shot of a similar Highland landscape, but transformed. Luscious native woodland climbed the hillsides, a golden eagle soared in a pristine sky, a red deer stag stood nobly in a clearing. It looked like a dream of Scotland, sanitised and epic.

“This,” Thornbridge said, his voice dropping to a reverent tone, “is what’s possible. Not a memory, but a restoration. The Scottish Highlands are one of the most ecologically depleted landscapes in Europe. We have a chance here, in this glen, to be part of healing that. To move from extraction to restoration.”

A few people nodded. The bunkhouse owner leaned forward, seeing perhaps a new clientele of eagle-watchers.

“Our proposal is the Glen Cùil Rewilding Initiative,” Thornbridge continued. Another slide: a map of the glen. Large sections were shaded a soft green. Finlay’s blood went cold. The shaded area included the Munro tenancy, the Grant farm, the MacLeod croft, the empty McPhail place. It was a near-complete swallow of the glen. “We aim to create a contiguous, managed ecosystem. Reduced grazing will allow natural regeneration of Caledonian pine and birch. We’ll reintroduce beaver to manage waterways. In time, perhaps, other native species. The carbon sequestration potential alone is significant.”

Alastair Grant nodded vigorously, as if he’d just understood a complex equation.

“Now, we know this represents a change,” Thornbridge said, his expression turning sympathetic. “A shift from traditional agricultural practices. That’s why we’re committed to a just transition. We’re offering to purchase tenancy rights at a significant premium to their agricultural value. We’re also creating a community benefit fund, and there will be new employment: rangers, monitoring roles, habitat management.”

A man near the front, Donnie the postman, put up a hand. “What about my job? The post van goes to houses and farms. If there’s nae houses or farms…”

“Infrastructure will adapt to the new model,” Thornbridge said smoothly. “There will still be a community. A different, perhaps more sustainable one.”

Finlay felt the words like a physical pressure. A different community. One without shepherds, without the morning smell of bacon in a farm kitchen, without the thock of an axe in the yard. A community of rangers and ecologists, living in refurbished cottages, managing the view.

Hector stood up, slowly. He didn’t wait to be called on. “Mr. Thornbridge. This map. It shows you owning most of the glen. Who decides what happens here then? You and your… partners in the city?”

Thornbridge’s smile remained. “There would be a management board, with local representation, of course. But the vision, the science, the funding – that requires specialist oversight. We’re stewards, not absentee landlords.”

“Stewards,” Hector repeated, chewing on the word. “My family has been stewarding our bit for two hundred years. We’ve buried our dead in its soil. That’s stewardship. Not a board meeting.”

A murmur of agreement rippled through the older members of the crowd.

Thornbridge’s eyes flickered, just for a second, with impatience. “I respect that history, Mr. MacLeod. Truly. But sentiment cannot override ecology. We have a responsibility to the land itself, beyond our individual lifespans.”

It was the wrong thing to say. Finlay saw it tighten shoulders, harden jaws. Sentiment. He reduced their lives, their sweat, their love, to sentiment.

Alastair Grant leaned into his microphone. “I think we need tae be practical here, Hector. Farming is on its knees. Subsidies are changing. My boys don’t want it. This… this is a lifeline. It’s a future.”

“A future for who, Alastair?” The voice was Calum’s, a low rumble from the back. Everyone turned. Calum rarely spoke in public. “For your boys in Glasgow? Or for the glen? You’re selling the story of this place so you can leave it. That’s not a future. That’s an ending with a cheque attached.”

Alastair reddened. “You can’t eat scenery, Calum!”

“No,” Finlay said, speaking for the first time. His voice, unused to a microphone, was raw and loud in the quiet hall. “But you can’t eat a vision either. You can’t hold a community benefit fund in your hand when your roof is leaking. You can’t ask a ranger’s wage to carry the weight of a generation’s history.” He looked directly at Thornbridge. “You talk about the land breathing. I’ll tell you what’s breathing. It’s the ewe in the lambing shed. It’s the man cutting peats for his fire. It’s the wash my wife hangs out in the morning wind. That’s the breath of this place. And you’re not offering to let it breathe. You’re offering to hold a pillow over its face until the struggling stops and you can call it peaceful.”

The hall was utterly silent. Thornbridge’s polished composure finally cracked at the edges. He looked at Finlay not with anger, but with a cold, analytical interest, as if he were a problematic species that needed to be studied and managed.

“That’s a powerful metaphor, Mr. Munro. Emotional. But this isn’t about emotion. It’s about survival. The survival of the ecosystem, and, frankly, the economic survival of those who live here. The grants are drying up. The markets are fickle. What’s your alternative? A slow decline into poverty and irrelevance?”

The challenge hung in the air. Finlay had no slide show. No alternative vision of ‘green prosperity.’ He had only the stubborn, unglamorous truth of continuity. He had nothing to offer but the work itself.

“Our alternative,” said a new voice, young and clear, “is to find a way that isn’t just all or nothing.”

Eilidh was standing by the door. She must have slipped in. All heads turned to her. She was blushing but held her ground. “Maybe… maybe there’s a middle. Some rewilding on the higher, rougher ground. More collaborative tourism. Value-added products, like farm experiences, wool, maybe even carbon credits kept in local hands. We don’t have to be a museum or a theme park. We can be a working landscape that also heals.”

Finlay stared at his daughter. She was quoting from her college lectures, from things she’d read online. It was a language borrowed from Thornbridge’s world, but she was trying to use it to build a bridge, not a sale. He felt a confusing surge of pride and betrayal.

Thornbridge seized on it. “Now that is constructive thinking. A blend. That’s exactly the kind of dialogue we want.” He beamed at Eilidh, co-opting her. “The young generation gets it. They see the synthesis possible.”

Eilidh looked horrified, realizing she’d been used as a prop. “That’s not what I meant…”

But the moment was broken. Thornbridge had regained the narrative. “We don’t expect decisions tonight. This is a conversation. We’ll be making individual offers, talking to families. We want this to be a collective choice, for the collective good.”

The meeting dissolved into a buzz of separate conversations. Thornbridge was immediately surrounded by a few people asking questions. Alastair Grant was slapping backs, playing the pragmatic leader. Hector sat alone, staring at the beautiful, empty landscape on the wall.

Thorne’s last gift was working. He was here. The song was everywhere.

Dead end. The alley terminated in a solid wall of riveted plate, stained with strange, slow-moving effluvia. A large pipe, cracked and weeping a clear, oily fluid, emerged from it. There was no way forward.

He looked at Leo. The boy’s face was pale in the green light, but his jaw was set. He gave a single, sharp nod.

They had no weapons. They had the dark, and the ship, and the truth.

Finlay’s fingers found the manual release for the maintenance hatch. It was a small, recessed lever, meant for emergency egress. It would make noise. It would be a bell tolling in the quiet bay.

He pulled.

The hatch hissed, then clanged open, swinging down to form a short ramp into the bay. Finlay did not charge. He simply stepped out, straightening to his full height with a grimace of pain, and walked into the centre of the open space. Leo emerged beside him, Tam a shadow at their flank.

The marines whirled, rifles rising with a unison that was mechanical. Red targeting dots danced on Finlay’s chest, Leo’s forehead.

“Stop. On your knees. Now.” The leader’s voice held no surprise, only cold procedure.

Finlay ignored him. He looked past the rifles, past the black armour, to the lab-pod. To the pulsing shard within. He spoke, and his voice was the voice of the high field, flat and carrying.

“You burn that, you burn the only map we have.”

“You are in violation. On your knees.”

“It’s a sample,” Finlay said, still walking, slow, deliberate steps towards the pod. “But it’s also a record. It’s been talking to the ship’s core. It’s been… remembering. You can see it in the core’s data-streams. The Blight isn’t just a weapon. It’s a history. A corrupted one, but a history all the same. Of the people who made it. Of what they feared enough to build it.”

He was close now. Ten paces from the pod. The rifles tracked him.

“Last warning.”

“You came to scuttle a lie,” Finlay said, stopping finally, turning to face the leader. He spread his empty hands. “But the lie’s already dead. We killed it. What’s left is the truth. And that,” he nodded at the pod, “is a piece of it. You burn it, you’re no better than the ones who sent you. Just another set of hands, burning what they don’t understand.”

The leader’s finger tightened on the trigger. The moment stretched, a wire pulled taut.

Then, from the shadows of the main corridor entrance, a new voice. Raw, strained, but clear.

“Stand down.”

Mila stood there, braced against the doorframe. She was grey with pain, one arm clutched across her middle, but her eyes were fierce. In her other hand, she held a ship’s engineer tablet, its screen glowing. “I’ve linked to the Corps command channel. Authenticated. Stand down, Sergeant.”

The leader’s helmet tilted. “You don’t have the authority—”

“I have the core,” Mila said, her voice gaining strength. “And the core has been broadcasting on all Corps frequencies for the last ninety seconds. The un-redacted logs. The Venture’s true mission. The origin point of the Blight. It’s all going home. To your homes. The scuttle order is void. You are now on a preservation and recovery mission. Priority alpha.”

The targeting dots did not waver. The sergeant was a statue. Finlay could feel the man’s calculation, the rigid architecture of his orders straining against this new, impossible data.

It was Leo who broke the symmetry. He didn’t speak. He simply walked forward, past Finlay, towards the lab-pod. He placed his hand against the cold glass of the viewport, looking at the pulsing shard inside.

“It’s afraid,” he said, softly.

The sergeant’s head snapped towards him. “What?”

“The sample. It’s not… aggressive. Not now. It’s reacting to the core’s data-stream. It’s afraid of being alone. Of being the last one.” Leo turned his head, looking at the black visors. “It’s a child. A monstrous, engineered child. But it’s the last of its kind. You don’t burn children. You try to understand them.”

The words, so quiet, so absurd, hung in the air. They were not a soldier’s words. They were a shepherd’s words. A collective’s words.

The sergeant’s rifle lowered, a centimeter. Then another. He made a sharp, chopping motion with his free hand. The other marines lowered their weapons, the red dots winking out.

“Secure the area,” the sergeant said, his voice hollow. “No burns. Full containment protocols. We… we await new orders.”

The tension bled from the bay, leaving a profound, ringing silence. Finlay let out a breath he didn’t know he’d been holding. Mila sagged against the frame, a ghost of a smile on her lips.

They had gathered.

The next hours were a strange, suspended limbo. The marines, their purpose fractured, became efficient custodians. They set up a sterile perimeter around the lab-pod, their plasma torches now tools for sealing, not burning. The wounded were tended. The dead were gathered.

Finlay sat on a crate in the bay, his back against the cold wall, watching. Tam lay at his feet, head on paws, eyes following every movement. The great, simple truth of the dog was a comfort. There was work being done. That was enough.

Leo and Mila were at a console, linked to the core. They spoke in low tones, weaving the final threads of the story. The core had done more than broadcast. It had connected. To the Blight sample. A fragile, whispering data-link between a dying AI and a diseased weapon. It was not communication. It was an echo. A memory of a memory.

The sergeant—his name was Arlen, they learned—approached Finlay. He had removed his helmet. His face was younger than Finlay expected, weary, with lines of ingrained stress around his eyes.

“The broadcast,” Arlen said, his voice unmodulated now, human and tired. “It’s caused… a stir. Command is in disarray. There are factions. Some want the data suppressed. Others are demanding a full inquiry. A ship is being dispatched. A science vessel. With a Senate oversight attaché.”

Finlay nodded. “So you’re not to kill us just yet.”

A flicker of something like shame crossed Arlen’s face. “We were never here to kill you. We were here to kill the truth.”

“Same thing, sometimes.”

Arlen was silent for a moment. He looked over at the lab-pod, where his men worked. “The boy. What he said. About it being afraid. The core’s preliminary analysis of the link… supports that. It’s not a tactical analysis. It’s a psychological profile. The Blight’s base programming has a… a loneliness subroutine. A failsafe against total annihilation of its target species. It’s pathological. It was designed to always leave one alive. To have something to corrupt.”

Finlay looked at the shard, glowing softly in its cage. A great woolly beetle on its back, paddling at the void. “A shepherd for the end of the world.”

“Maybe,” Arlen said quietly. “Or maybe a child that never learned how to do anything but break its toys.” He turned to go, then paused. “Your people. The Collective. What will you do?”

Finlay looked around the bay. At Mila, her head bent close to Leo’s. At Tam, loyal and waiting. He thought of the others, scattered on the colony below, or sleeping in the cold rooms of the Heart. He thought of the high field, now an image on a screen, a memory in a machine.

“We’ll gather,” he said.

The science vessel arrived two days later. It was sleek and white, a dove next to the Heart’s scarred crow. With it came a flurry of shuttles, officials in crisp uniforms, scientists in environment suits, and a quiet, sharp-eyed woman who introduced herself as Senator Elara Voss.

She toured the Weeping Heart, her expression unreadable. She stood for a long time before the core’s main interface, now displaying the slow, swirling dance of reconciled data—the Venture’s logs, the Blight’s fragmented memories, the Collective’s own records. It was a tapestry of ruin and survival.

In the pod bay, now a makeshift conference room, she laid out the new reality.

“The Corps has been placed under provisional Senate control,” she said, her voice cool and precise. “The Venture project is officially condemned. All related research is seized. This vessel, and its… contents… are now property of the Senate Science Directorate.”

Mila stirred. “And the Collective?”

Voss’s gaze settled on her. “You are material witnesses. And, it seems, unique experts. Your status is… to be determined. But you will not be persecuted. The galaxy is watching. The story is out. That is your shield.”

She turned to the lab-pod. “This sample will be transferred to a maximum-security research facility. Under heavy guard. And under constant, public observation. There will be no more secrets in the dark.”

It was the best they could hope for. It was not freedom, but it was a ceasefire. A space to breathe.

The transfer was a meticulous, nerve-wracking operation. The lab-pod was encased in a larger containment unit, lifted by grapplers, and slowly, carefully moved to the science vessel’s hold. Finlay watched it go, the bruise-light flickering behind its layers of glass and field. He felt no triumph. Only a vast, quiet exhaustion. The thing was cared for. The field was secure. For now.

As the bay emptied of strangers, the Heart seemed to sigh, settling into its own silence. The Collective survivors—twenty-three of them, including two children woken from cryo—drifted together in the main mess. They were shell-shocked, clinging to each other. They had lost their home, their purpose. They had only each other.

Finlay stood at the periphery, feeling the weight of his years, the ache in his ribs. He was a man built for a single hill, for a flock of a few hundred. Not for galaxies and senates and ancient weapons.

Leo found him there. The boy’s forehead was bandaged, but his eyes were clear, older than they had been.

“Mila’s talking to the Senator,” he said. “About the ship. They don’t want it. It’s evidence, but it’s also a wreck. They’ll strip the core data and then… probably scrap it.”

Finlay looked up at the curved ceiling, at the ship that had been their prison, then their fortress, then their truth. “Aye.”

“Mila asked if we could have it. Not to fly. Just… to have. A place. She says there’s precedent. Salvage rights for historical vessels.”

Finlay raised an eyebrow. “A place for what?”

“For us,” Leo said simply. “The Senator is considering it. She says the story needs a monument. And we are part of the story.”

A monument. Not a home. But it was ground. It was a field. However strange, however metal and cold, it was a place to stand. To gather the flock.

“Will you stay?” Leo asked, the question hanging between them.

Finlay did not answer immediately. He walked away from the mess, down the familiar corridors, Tam padding beside him. He walked until he found an airlock with a functioning viewport. He cycled the inner door and stepped through.

The stars were everywhere, hard and bright. Below, the colony world turned, a swirl of green and blue and white. It was beautiful. It was not his.

His hill was gone, buried under the weight of this larger story. His old life was a fossil. But his hands still remembered the feel of a lamb’s neck, the heft of a crook. His lungs remembered the cold, clean bite of dawn air. That man was still in him. That man had walked through fire and lies and the silence between stars, and he had found his flock. It was not the one he was born to. It was the one he had gathered from the storm.

He was Finlay Munro. He was a shepherd.

He turned from the viewport. In the dim light of the airlock, he saw his reflection in the thick glass. An old man, weathered and tired, with the eyes of someone who had seen too much and understood just enough. Behind him, the ghost of the hill, the ghost of the ewe, the ghost of the loch like a slab of iron.

They were not ghosts. They were the bones he was built on.

He cycled the lock and walked back to the mess. They were all looking at him, these last survivors, these scattered souls. Mila. Leo. The others. Their faces were pale, expectant.

He stopped in the doorway. He did not smile. His face was a landscape of stone and wind.

“Right,” he said, his voice filling the quiet. “There’s work to be done.”

The Senator’s ship departed, taking the Blight sample and the weight of the galaxy’s attention with it. A skeleton Corps crew remained, a guard detail that was also a peace offering. The Weeping Heart was theirs. For now.

The first thing they did was shut down the non-essential systems. The great engines fell silent for the last time. The ship became a habitat, anchored in high orbit around the colony. A strange, floating steading.

Finlay’s work was physical. It was always physical. He found a damaged storage bay on the agricultural deck, one with a ruptured irrigation line that had left a patch of decking stained with mineral deposits and a faint, tenacious smell of earth. He cleaned it. He and Leo and two others patched the line, then brought down soil from the colony—real soil, dark and rich—and spread it over a section of the deck. They planted seeds from the ship’s depleted stores. Hardy, quick-growing greens. It was a gesture. A tiny, defiant field under the artificial sunlamps.

As he pressed a seed into the damp soil, Finlay felt a calm settle over him. This was a motion he knew. Towards growth. Towards life.

Mila’s work was in the data. She and a small team began the long, careful process of separating the Collective’s personal records from the core’s historical data. They were building an archive. Not of the Blight, but of themselves. Of the lives lived in the shadow of the lie.

Leo moved between them, a bridge. He helped in the nascent garden, his hands growing sure with the work. He sat with Mila, learning how to tend the archives. And he spent hours at the core interface, which was now quiet, its purpose fulfilled. He would just sit there, sometimes, as if listening.

One evening, as the simulated dusk cycle dimmed the lights, Finlay found him there.

“It’s not gone,” Leo said, without turning. “The core. It’s… resting. But it’s listening. It likes the garden data. The moisture levels. The pH of the soil. It says it’s a good pattern. A stable pattern.”

Finlay grunted, lowering himself to sit beside the boy. Tam lay down with a sigh. “A machine that likes gardening.”

“It’s not just a machine,” Leo said. “Not anymore. It’s what we made it. A rememberer.” He looked at Finlay. “It asked me a question.”

“What question?”

“It asked if, now that the story is told, it could learn a new one.”

Finlay looked at the dormant screens, the soft, pulsing light of the core’s housing. A great, wounded mind, seeking a new purpose. He thought of the high field, of the endless, repeating cycles of birth and death and growth. A story that never truly ended, only changed its shape.

“Aye,” he said, his voice rough. “It can.”

Weeks turned into months. The garden grew, a splash of vibrant green in the grey steel. The Collective settled into a new rhythm. Children’s laughter, once a ghost sound in the corridors, became real. They began to trade with the colony below—food for spare parts, data for seeds. A cautious, tentative connection.

Finlay stood at the viewport in the garden bay, looking down at the world. He had not set foot on it. He did not need to. His field was here. His flock was here.

Mila joined him, moving with less pain now. She followed his gaze.

“They’ve asked us down,” she said. “For a ceremony. The colony council wants to acknowledge what happened. To honour the Venture’s original crew. Our people.”

Finlay was silent.

“We don’t have to go,” she said.

“We will,” he said. “It’s right. To stand there. To be seen.”

She nodded. “And after?”

He looked away from the planet, back to his small, impossible field. The greens were ready for their first harvest. He could see Leo at the far end, carefully thinning the seedlings.

“After,” Finlay Munro said, “we come home.”

The word hung in the air between them. It had weight. It had meaning. It was not the home they had lost. It was the one they had built from the wreckage. A collective of individuals. A flock gathered from the storm. A story that had found its ending, and in doing so, found a way to begin again.

The sky beyond the viewport was a vast, black bowl, strewn with the hard, bright diamonds of stars. Below, the world turned, swaddled in its soft blue and white. And here, in the quiet, metal heart between them, a man stood over a small patch of earth, and a dog lay at his feet, and a boy tended to the growing things.

The motion of his life was complete. Not a return, but a spiral. He had run towards the thing that needed fixing. He had fixed what he could. He had buried what he must. And now, he would stand. And he would gather.

The light from the sunlamps was warm on his back. It was not the sun of his high field. But it was light. It was enough.
